A case study on Beacon, a research platform that scrapes, scores, and analyzes every listed Indian company using quantitative frameworks and a multi-agent LLM pipeline.

Built as a portfolio project structured around the real needs of a boutique investment firm. Public at [github.com/dewanggogte/beacon](https://github.com/dewanggogte/beacon). Technical deep dive on the [blog](/blog/projects/building-beacon).

## The problem

Boutique PMSes, family offices, and retail investment platforms in India all share the same problem. The public market has 5,300+ listed companies. Traditional analyst teams can only deeply cover 30-50. Everything else gets ignored, screened out by crude filters, or covered sporadically when a name pops up in the news.

The real alpha sits in the companies nobody's looking at. Small-cap mispricings. Mid-cap governance problems hiding in plain sight. Quality compounders outside the Nifty 500. Firms without the headcount to cover the full market are systematically missing this.

The conventional solution is to buy expensive screener databases (Bloomberg, Capitaline) and write SQL. That tells you which companies rank well on ROE. It doesn't tell you which ones are actually worth owning. Which ones have promoter red flags. Which ones have hidden segment risk. How any of this changes across macro regimes.

A platform needs three things to solve this properly. Full-market coverage (not the top 500). A defensible scoring methodology (not "high ROE"). Qualitative analysis layered on top of the quant (because financials alone miss governance and business quality).

None of this scales with humans.

## What was built

Two-layer architecture. Every company goes through Layer 1. A qualified subset goes through Layer 2.

**Layer 1: quantitative scoring.**

Every company gets scored on 21 metrics across 5 dimensions: valuation (25% weight), quality (30%), governance (20%), safety (15%), momentum (10%). Sector-specific adjustments for IT, Banking, Pharma, Manufacturing, and FMCG (because a bank's 15% ROE is excellent and a FMCG company's 15% ROE is mediocre; the same score across sectors is meaningless).

Four framework evaluators run in parallel:
- Buffett's 10-criteria checklist (economic moat, consistent earnings, low capex, etc.)
- Graham's defensive screen (debt-to-equity, earnings stability, dividend record)
- Lynch's 6-category classification (fast grower, stalwart, slow grower, cyclical, turnaround, asset play)
- Pabrai's risk assessment (6 factors)

Eight hard disqualifiers flag companies regardless of score. Consecutive losses, promoter stake decline greater than 10pp a year, related-party transaction concentration above threshold, etc.

Composite score is classification-aware. Fast growers weight Lynch's framework at 30%. Stalwarts weight dividend safety and quality higher. A single one-size-fits-all composite misses the point.

Final classification: strong_long (>=80), potential_long (>=65), neutral (>=40), potential_short (>=20), strong_avoid (<20).

**Layer 2: multi-agent LLM analysis.**

A 4-agent pipeline with structured chain-of-thought prompts:

- **AG1, Fundamentals.** Business quality, competitive moats, growth trajectory.
- **AG2, Governance.** Promoter track record, related-party transactions, capital allocation history.
- **AG3, Risk (Devil's Advocate).** Must surface at least 2 material risks per company. Deliberately adversarial.
- **AG4, Synthesis.** Final assessment with macro regime context and peer comparison. Has authority to override the Layer 1 quant classification.

AG1 and AG4 each produce independent scores (0-100), not just adjustments to the quant score. Both the quant and AG4 classifications are preserved in the dashboard with attribution for each, so the user always sees where the opinion came from.

**Funnel tiering controls LLM cost.**

Top 100 quant-ranked companies get full AG1 to AG4. The next 500 get AG1 screening, and the top 100 from that cohort get promoted to full analysis. Remaining 4,500+ get Layer 1 quant coverage only. This concentrates LLM spend where depth matters while keeping full-market coverage intact.

**Post-validation catches hallucinations.**

LLM outputs get cross-checked against the actual quantitative data. If AG4 writes "strong promoter track record" but the data shows a 15pp promoter stake decline in 3 years, the system flags the contradiction and down-ranks the claim. This design treats LLM output as a separate signal to be validated, not as ground truth.

**Divergence watcher for human review.**

When quant and AG4 disagree significantly on classification, an email alert goes out. The interesting companies are always the ones the two layers disagree on. Humans review those explicitly.

## The hard parts

**Scraping without getting blocked.**
Screener.in serves 13 years of financials, ratios, shareholding, and peer comparisons per company. Scraping 5,300 companies sustainably means careful rate limiting, rotating user agents, and graceful handling of HTML structure changes. I used native fetch + Cheerio, no headless browser needed. The whole scrape finishes in a few hours.

**Sector-specific weighting.**
The same financial metric means completely different things across sectors. A bank with 15% ROE is excellent. A FMCG company with 15% ROE is mediocre. I built per-sector weight adjustments and metric substitutions (NIM for banks instead of operating margin, asset turnover for retail, etc.).

**Classification-aware scoring.**
Lynch's framework splits companies into 6 categories, and each deserves different weighting. A fast grower's composite should emphasize momentum and growth. A stalwart should emphasize quality and dividend safety. I built the entire composite engine to vary by Lynch category. Different weight tables, different disqualifiers, different thresholds.

**LLM hallucinations on financial data.**
Claude and Qwen are great at reasoning. They also confidently make up numbers. Post-validation became essential. Every numerical claim AG1-AG4 makes gets cross-referenced against actual data. Contradictions override the LLM. The dual-scoring design (AG1 and AG4 as independent scores, not adjustments) lets the system treat LLM output as a separate input rather than as truth.

**Making 8 dashboard pages useful, not pretty.**
Most financial dashboards bury the signal in data tables. Beacon shows conviction badges, framework-specific breakdowns, agent reasoning visible per claim, and backtest results alongside the live picks. The design is warm, minimal, light-theme. Deliberately not a Bloomberg terminal.

## The numbers

- 5,300+ companies scored, end-to-end pipeline refreshable on demand
- 21 quantitative metrics, 4 investing frameworks, 8 hard disqualifiers
- 4 specialized LLM agents with funnel tiering (top 100 + next 100 promoted = 200 full analyses per run)
- Post-validation catches roughly 1 in 5 LLM claims that contradict quant data in testing
