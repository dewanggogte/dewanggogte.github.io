## The problem

Most LLM pipelines run into the same three problems as they scale:

- **Costs balloon.** Run a multi-agent analysis over 5,000 items and the bill gets ugly fast.
- **Models hallucinate.** They confidently invent numbers, misattribute facts, and agree with themselves on things that aren't true.
- **Generic scoring loses signal.** A one-size-fits-all model treats everything the same. In the real world, the same metric means different things in different contexts.

These three problems compound. You can't just throw more compute at a hallucinating system, and you can't trust a cheap-and-fast pipeline to be right.

Beacon is built around solving those three problems first, with equity research as the worked example. The Indian public market has 5,300+ listed companies — too many for any human team to cover, and a clean stress test for all three failure modes. The same patterns that make Beacon work apply to any domain where you need to score, rank, and deepen analysis across a large dataset.

## What was built

Two-layer architecture. Every item goes through Layer 1. A qualified subset goes through Layer 2.

**Layer 1: deterministic pre-filter**<br/>
A fast, rule-based scoring pass over the entire dataset before any LLM touches it. In Beacon's case, every company gets scored on 21 metrics across 5 dimensions: valuation (25% weight), quality (30%), governance (20%), safety (15%), momentum (10%). Sector-specific adjustments because a bank's 15% ROE is excellent and a FMCG company's 15% ROE is mediocre. The same context-awareness applies in any domain.

Four domain-specific evaluators run in parallel. In this case, classic investing frameworks — in another domain they'd be your own scoring rubrics:

- Buffett's 10-criteria checklist (economic moat, consistent earnings, low capex)
- Graham's defensive screen (debt-to-equity, earnings stability, dividend record)
- Lynch's 6-category classification (fast grower, stalwart, slow grower, cyclical, turnaround, asset play)
- Pabrai's risk assessment (6 factors)

Eight hard disqualifiers act as rule-based filters that flag items regardless of score — consecutive losses, promoter stake decline greater than 10pp a year, related-party transaction concentration above threshold, etc. The pattern: cheap, deterministic rules catch the obvious failures before LLMs ever see them.

Composite score is classification-aware — scoring weights vary by item type. Fast growers weight Lynch's framework at 30%. Stalwarts weight dividend safety and quality higher. The same logic applies anywhere one-size-fits-all scoring would lose signal.

Final classification: strong_long (>=80), potential_long (>=65), neutral (>=40), potential_short (>=20), strong_avoid (below 20).

**Layer 2: multi-agent LLM analysis**<br/>
A 4-agent pipeline with structured chain-of-thought prompts. Each agent has a narrow role:

- **AG1, Fundamentals.** Business quality, competitive moats, growth trajectory.
- **AG2, Governance.** Promoter track record, related-party transactions, capital allocation.
- **AG3, Risk (Devil's Advocate).** Must surface at least 2 material risks per item. Deliberately adversarial — its job is to find reasons not to.
- **AG4, Synthesis.** Final assessment with macro context and peer comparison. Has authority to override Layer 1.

The adversarial agent is the single most transferable idea here. Most LLM pipelines have all the agents agreeing with each other. AG3 exists specifically to push back, and it changes the output quality dramatically.

AG1 and AG4 each produce independent scores (0-100), not just adjustments to the quant score. Both Layer 1 and AG4 classifications are preserved with attribution, so the user always sees where the opinion came from.

**Funnel tiering controls cost**<br/>
The headline cost-control pattern. The top 100 Layer 1 items get full AG1 to AG4. The next 500 get AG1 screening, and the top 100 from that cohort get promoted to full analysis. The remaining 4,500+ get Layer 1 coverage only. Concentrates LLM spend where depth actually matters while keeping full-dataset coverage intact. The same architecture works for any large dataset — only the tier sizes change.

**Post-validation catches hallucinations**<br/>
The headline reliability pattern. LLM outputs get cross-checked against the actual underlying data. If AG4 writes "strong promoter track record" but the data shows a 15pp promoter stake decline in 3 years, the system flags the contradiction and down-ranks the claim. This treats LLM output as a separate signal to be validated, not as ground truth.

**Divergence watcher for human review**<br/>
When Layer 1 and AG4 disagree significantly, an alert fires. The interesting items are usually the ones the two layers disagree on — that's where humans should spend their time. Same pattern works anywhere you have a deterministic signal and an LLM signal that should mostly agree.

## The hard parts

**Ingesting messy upstream data reliably**<br/>
Screener.in serves 13 years of financials, ratios, shareholding, and peer comparisons per company. Scraping 5,300 companies sustainably means careful rate limiting, rotating user agents, and graceful handling of HTML structure changes. Native fetch + Cheerio, no headless browser needed. The whole scrape finishes in a few hours. Same engineering applies to any large-scale data ingestion job.

**Context-aware scoring**<br/>
The same metric means completely different things across contexts. A bank with 15% ROE is excellent. A FMCG company with 15% ROE is mediocre. Built per-sector weight adjustments and metric substitutions (NIM for banks instead of operating margin, asset turnover for retail). Any classification or scoring system that ignores context is leaving signal on the table.

**Composite scoring that varies by item type**<br/>
Lynch's framework splits companies into 6 categories, and each deserves different weighting. A fast grower's composite should emphasize momentum and growth. A stalwart should emphasize quality and dividend safety. The whole composite engine varies by category — different weight tables, different disqualifiers, different thresholds.

**LLM hallucinations on factual data**<br/>
Claude and Qwen are great at reasoning. They also confidently make up numbers. Post-validation became essential. Every numerical claim AG1-AG4 makes gets cross-referenced against actual data. Contradictions override the LLM. The dual-scoring design (AG1 and AG4 as independent scores, not adjustments) lets the system treat LLM output as a separate input rather than as truth.

**Making output legible to non-engineer reviewers**<br/>
Most analytical dashboards bury the signal in data tables. Beacon shows conviction badges, framework-specific breakdowns, agent reasoning visible per claim, and validation flags alongside the picks. Warm, minimal, light-theme. Deliberately not a Bloomberg terminal. Whoever's reviewing the output shouldn't need to be an engineer to use it.
